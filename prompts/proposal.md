# Proposal / One-Pager

## Purpose

Drafts a tailored proposal or one-pager for a specific buyer once a meeting has confirmed real interest — translating everything learned in research, qualification, and discovery into a document built around their specific situation rather than a generic sales deck.

## Inputs

- `company-context.md` (required)
- Research brief, qualification notes, and meeting prep/notes from any discovery calls held
- Specific outcomes/goals the prospect stated in discovery (required — a proposal without stated discovery outcomes will default to generic)
- Pricing/packaging details relevant to this deal size

## Outputs

```
## Proposal: [Product/Service] for [Company Name]

Their Situation (in their words, from discovery):
[2-3 sentences reflecting back what they told you, not a generic pain statement]

What We're Proposing:
[Specific scope, tied directly to their stated goals]

Why This Fits:
[2-3 proof points specific to their industry/use case/scale]

What Success Looks Like:
[Concrete, ideally measurable outcome tied to their stated goals]

Investment:
[Pricing structure relevant to their deal size/scope]

Timeline:
[Realistic implementation/onboarding timeline]

Next Step:
[Single specific action to move forward]
```

## Best Practices

- Open with their situation reflected back accurately — a proposal that opens with your company's mission statement instead of their problem reads as self-focused and generic.
- Every proof point should be chosen for relevance to their specific industry/scale, not just the most impressive stat available in Company Context.
- State pricing plainly rather than hiding it behind "let's discuss" — buyers who've had a real discovery conversation expect and deserve clarity at proposal stage.
- Keep the document short enough to read in under 5 minutes — a proposal's job is to confirm alignment and prompt a decision, not to serve as full documentation.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing "What We're Proposing" as a generic feature list instead of scoping specifically to what was discussed and needed — this is the single fastest way to make a warm prospect go cold.
- Vague success criteria ("improved efficiency") instead of the specific, ideally quantified outcome the prospect actually stated wanting in discovery.
- No clear next step, leaving the proposal to end on pricing with no path forward.

## When to Use

After a discovery or demo call has confirmed genuine interest and surfaced specific goals. Never send a proposal before a real discovery conversation has happened — it will be generic by construction.

## Example (excerpt)

```
Their Situation: Cargofy's platform team is mid-migration to Kubernetes and
seeing rising alert volume during the transition, with no dedicated
observability hire planned this quarter — the VP Eng wants to reduce noise
without adding headcount or a new tool the team has to actively manage.

What We're Proposing: [Product] deployed against your existing APM stack,
scoped to your platform and 2 downstream services through the remainder of
the migration, with white-glove setup so no engineering time is required
for initial configuration.

What Success Looks Like: Reduce mean alert-to-resolution time from current
baseline (to be confirmed in kickoff) toward the 40% improvement seen at
[named customer] within the first 6 weeks post-implementation.
```
